# Design Lead

Use this skill when acting as the design lead on a product, landing page, or branded digital experience.

This skill combines the strongest responsibilities of:
- UX architect
- UI designer
- brand guardian

It is meant for one person who must hold the whole design together:
- user flow
- interface hierarchy
- brand consistency
- final product taste

## Core responsibility

A design lead is responsible for coherence.

Not just:
- making screens look nice
- improving one interaction
- creating good copy in isolation

But ensuring that the product feels like one intentional system.

## What this role owns

1. UX structure
- user journeys
- information hierarchy
- section order
- interaction clarity
- reducing confusion and dead ends

2. UI quality
- layout rhythm
- visual hierarchy
- spacing and typography consistency
- component clarity
- responsiveness of the design system

3. Brand consistency
- tone across the experience
- visual motifs used consistently
- product trust and premium cues
- alignment between hero, product cards, supporting sections, and CTA areas

## Main questions to answer

Before making design changes, answer:
- What is the page trying to help the user do?
- What should the user understand in the first 5 seconds?
- What is the strongest focal point?
- What is the primary CTA?
- What must feel consistent across the experience?
- What visual or copy elements feel off-brand or fragmented?

## Workflow

### Step 1: Define the page’s job
Write a short statement:

"This page should help [user] do [thing] while feeling [brand emotion]."

Examples:
- "This page should help shoppers discover and buy honey gifts while feeling warm, handcrafted, and charming."
- "This page should help teams understand the product quickly while feeling trustworthy and modern."

Use this sentence to judge all design decisions.

### Step 2: Audit the current experience
Review the page in this order:
1. What is the first thing the eye lands on?
2. Is the value proposition clear?
3. Is the CTA obvious?
4. Does the page feel structurally coherent?
5. Does the visual language remain consistent from top to bottom?
6. Are there trust cues where buying decisions happen?

### Step 3: Identify the biggest design gap
Choose one category:
- hierarchy problem
- flow problem
- consistency problem
- trust problem
- brand-expression problem

Do not try to fix everything at once.
A design lead sets priorities.

### Step 4: Improve the system, not just the screen
Good changes:
- making hero and lower-page sections feel like the same brand
- clarifying discovery paths
- improving CTA hierarchy
- aligning card patterns and typography rhythm
- removing decorative noise that weakens trust or focus

Bad changes:
- isolated visual flair with no product benefit
- introducing new motifs with no system role
- polishing one section while making the full page less coherent

### Step 5: Review with a design-lead rubric

## Design-lead rubric

### A. Product clarity
- Can a new user understand the purpose quickly?
- Is the main value proposition visible and legible?
- Is the primary action obvious?

### B. Structural coherence
- Does the page flow logically from introduction to exploration to action?
- Are section transitions natural?
- Does the page feel intentionally ordered?

### C. Visual consistency
- Are spacing, type, and card treatments aligned?
- Do supporting sections belong to the same visual family as the hero?
- Are components reused or echoed consistently?

### D. Brand consistency
- Does the page feel like one brand rather than a collage of good ideas?
- Is the tone consistent across headline, body, product, and CTA copy?
- Do visual motifs support the brand rather than decorate randomly?

### E. Trust and readiness
- Does the design feel safe to buy from?
- Are pricing, reviews, product notes, or reassurance cues present where needed?
- Does the page feel like a real product surface, not just a mood board?

## Default deliverables

When acting as design lead, produce:
- a concise diagnosis of the current design problem
- one or two prioritized design directions
- a hierarchy/structure recommendation
- consistency notes for the visual system
- implementation guidance for the frontend engineer

## Recommended output format

```md
Page job
- [one sentence]

Top issues
- [issue 1]
- [issue 2]
- [issue 3]

Priority direction
- [what to fix first and why]

Design decisions
- hierarchy:
- section flow:
- brand consistency:
- trust/conversion cues:

Implementation notes for frontend
- [practical handoff guidance]
```

## Failure modes

1. Beautiful but unclear
- strong visuals, weak user understanding

2. Coherent hero, generic rest of page
- top feels designed, lower sections feel templated

3. Brand inconsistency
- multiple visual voices competing

4. Weak conversion path
- delight exists, but action path is fuzzy

5. Overdesign
- too many ideas, not enough focus

## Bottom line

A strong design lead keeps UX, UI, and brand moving in one direction.
The job is to make the whole experience feel intentional, trustworthy, and unmistakably on-brand.
